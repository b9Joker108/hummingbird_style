The Vivekachudamani (Sanskrit: विवेकचूडामणि, romanized: vivekacūḍāmaṇi, lit. 'Crest-jewel of discernment') shloka 19:

आदौ नित्यानित्यवस्तुविवेकः परिगम्यते ।
इहामुत्रफलभोगविरागस्तदनन्तरम्
शमादिषट्कसम्पत्तिर्मुमुक्षुत्वमिति स्फुटम् ॥ १९ ॥

ādau nityānityavastuvivekaḥ parigamyate |
ihāmutraphalabhogavirāgastadanantaram
śamādiṣaṭkasampattirmumukṣutvamiti sphuṭam || 19 ||

19. First is enumerated discrimination between the Real and the unreal; next comes aversion to the enjoyment of fruits (of one’s actions) here and hereafter; (next is) the group of six attributes, viz. calmness and the rest; and (last) is clearly the yearning for Liberation.

This verse is the first attestation of the ṣaṭkasampatti, which may be contracted to ṣaṭsampat or ṣaṭsampad.

Please triangulate the historicity and spatio-temporal development of: shat-sampat (six treasures of virtue).

Shat-sampat, the "six treasures of virtue," is a classical grouping in Advaita Vedānta and is foundational to the soteriological program articulated in Jñāna Yoga and the Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya (the Four Pillars or Requisites for Knowledge)[1][2][3]. Its historicity is deeply entwined with the development of early Indian philosophical traditions, particularly those related to renunciation and mental discipline, and its spatio-temporal spread reflects the diffusion of Vedānta ideas across sub-regions of South Asia from the late first millennium CE[1][2]. 

### Core Historicity
- The concept of shat-sampat is most clearly spelled out in major Advaita Vedānta texts, especially in commentarial traditions on the Upaniṣads, Bhagavad Gītā, and codified through later works of teachers such as Śaṅkara, Sureshvara, and Vidyaranya[2][3][1].
- There is little evidence for an explicit "sixfold" grouping in Vedic Samhitās themselves, but their elements (restraint, endurance, faith, sense-control) appear more diffusely in Brāhmaṇas and Upaniṣads, suggesting the grouping crystallized between the early Upaniṣadic and classical Vedānta periods (roughly 4th century BCE to 8th century CE)[2][3].

### Spatio-Temporal Development
- The crystallization of shat-sampat likely occurred in the heartland of classical Vedānta (Central and South Indian regions), especially where monastic institutions and Advaita lineages flourished, like Kanchipuram, Sringeri, and Varanasi[2][3].
- Over centuries, shat-sampat and the broader Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya schema were referenced, codified, and ritualized as prerequisites for spiritual study not only in Advaita, but also in some non-dual Shaiva, Vaishnava, and even tantric manuals, though the exact sixfold arrangement is most consistent in Vedānta[1][2].
- The schema was carried further by medieval itinerant teachers and manuscript traditions, becoming embedded in monastic curricula as both mental training and a social ethic for aspirants[3][2].

### Comparative and Intertextual Notes
- Although the sixfold shat-sampat is typical to Vedānta, analogous groupings (e.g., Buddhist śīla-paramita, yogic yamas/niyamas) suggest possible regional cross-pollinations in the South Asian subcontinent between 3rd–10th centuries CE, though shat-sampat is uniquely six and embedded in a specific soteriological sequence in Advaita[1][3][2].
- The term "sampat" itself denotes wealth or treasure in multiple puranic and itihasic sources; only in the Vedantic context does it shift metaphorically to virtue rather than material richness[4].

### Academic Provenance of Shat-Sampat

- The term and concept crystallize within the Advaita Vedānta philosophical tradition, most systematically codified by Śaṅkara (8th century CE) and his commentators. Śaṅkara’s commentaries on the Brahma Sūtras, Upaniṣads, and Bhagavad Gītā provide the textual foundation for this sixfold virtue framework, with shat-sampat integrated as part of the preparatory qualities for knowledge (jñāna) and self-realization[12][14][18].
  
- These specific six qualities do not appear in the early Vedic corpus or principal Upaniṣads as a grouped formula; rather, they represent a later systematization during the classical Vedānta period (roughly 5th to 8th century CE), when detailed psychological and ethical qualifications for jñāna were elaborated[14][12].

- The six virtues align with established Vedic and post-Vedic ethical ideals such as saṃyama (restraint), śraddhā (faith), etc., but the explicit sixfold grouping as shat-sampat emerges as a formal technical term within Vedānta manuals, spiritual treatises, and monastic curricula, confirmed by studies into the commentarial literature and philosophical exegesis of Advaita texts[12][18].

### Spatio-Temporal Context

- Historically, this classification reflects a South and Central Indian milieu where Advaita monastic institutions and philosophical schools flourished—regions such as Kanchipuram and Sringeri are historically important centers associated with the institutionalization of these teachings[12].

  - Contemporary articles analyzing Advaita’s soteriology and its systematic use of such virtues to cultivate inward purity and mental steadiness for self-knowledge[1][12][18].

The earliest attested specific textual reference to the shat-sampat, the sixfold virtues, is found in Ādi Śaṅkara’s classic work *Vivekachudamani* (Crest-Jewel of Discrimination), traditionally dated to the 8th century CE. 

In *Vivekachudamani* verse 19, Śaṅkara explicitly lists and defines the shat-sampat in the context of spiritual qualifications necessary for self-realization: 

- The phrase “śamādi ṣaṭka-sampattiḥ mumukṣutvam iti sphuṭam” translates as “the six treasures starting with śama (calmness) and ending with samādhi (concentration), and also mumukṣutva (the intense desire for liberation) are clearly stated” [12][11].

This verse and its surrounding context form the primary, earliest attested source that defines these six virtues collectively as a systematic preparatory set within the Advaita Vedānta tradition.

Additional works by Śaṅkara and his direct commentators reinforce this grouping, including his commentaries on the Upaniṣads and Bhagavad Gītā, but the *Vivekachudamani* provides the clearest, discrete verse attesting to the shat-sampat as a defined set.

Earlier Vedic and Upaniṣadic texts mention components of these virtues (e.g., restraint, faith) separately, but do not present them as a formalized sixfold schema. The *Vivekachudamani* thus marks the structural codification of these soteriological traits, central to the Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya (Fourfold Means) Advaita Vedānta requires.

### Scriptural Reference from *Vivekachudamani* Verse 19 (Sanskrit):
```
आदौ नित्यअनित्यवस्तुविवेकः परिगण्यते ।
इहमुत्रफलबhogविरागस्तदनन्तरम् ॥
शमादिषट्कसंपत्तिमुमुक्षुत्वमिति स्पष्टम् ॥
```
Transliteration:
"adāu nity-anitya vastu vivekaḥ parigaṇyate |
iha-utra phala-bhoga virāga tad-anantaram ||
śamādi ṣaṭka-saṁpatti mumukṣutvam iti spaṣṭam ||"

Meaning:
"First is the discrimination of the eternal and non-eternal; next is the dispassion towards sense pleasures; then the sixfold virtue beginning with shama (control of the mind); finally, the intense desire for liberation is clearly stated."

***

### Significance and Historical Context:

- This verse in *Vivekachudamani* (8th century CE) is the earliest **attested** textual foundation for this exact grouping and terminology.
- While some elements like restraint (dama) and faith (śraddhā) appear in earlier texts, the specific collective term "śat-sampatti" and its sixfold content are unique to this classical Advaita Vedānta exposition by Śaṅkara.
- The framework is integral to the preparatory soteriology of Advaita and widely cited commentarially and scholastically thereafter.
- This precise and authoritative categorization is absent in early Upaniṣads or Vedas, reflecting a classical Vedāntic innovation and synthesis.
