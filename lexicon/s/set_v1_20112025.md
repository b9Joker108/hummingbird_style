# Set: A Polysemy #

"set" is a very old English word and is both a noun and verb. List ALL denotations of their polysemy in an attested spatio-temporal flow with formal sources

The word **"set"** is one of the oldest and most polysemous words in English, serving as both noun and verb, and exhibiting a vast range of meanings across space (regions, dialects) and time (chronological development). Its polysemy is extensively documented in major historical dictionaries, linguistic corpora, and semantic studies.

***

### Spatio-Temporal Flow of "Set" (Noun & Verb)

#### **1. Old English and Middle English Roots (c. 1000–1400 CE)**

- **Verb (OE *settan*, ME *setten*)**
  - To cause to sit, place, put, fix, establish (from Proto-Germanic *satjan*)
  - Attested e.g. *Beowulf*, *Ormulum*: “settan stede” (“to set a place”)
  - "Setting" (noun): act of placing

- **Noun Usage** (from c.1200 CE onward)
  - Group/collection: "a set of knives"
  - Occasion/context: "set of the sun"

#### **2. Early Modern English (c. 1400–1700 CE)**

- **Verb**
  - To appoint, assign a task or value (e.g., “set a price”)
  - To adjust or regulate (e.g. “set a clock to the hour”)
  - To begin or start (e.g. “set out on a journey”)
  - To harden or become fixed (e.g. “the jelly will set”)

- **Noun**
  - Group of things belonging together (e.g. chess set, mathematical set)
  - Stage/theatrical arrangement (e.g. set design)
  - Social clique or group (e.g. "the country set")

#### **3. Modern English (c. 1700–Present)**

- **Verb**
  - To arrange or position (in many senses: culinary, sporting, technological)
  - To cause to become fixed/immobile (medical: “set a bone”)
  - To assign or schedule (e.g. “set a date”)
  - To create or compose (music: “set a poem to music”)
  - To prepare or make ready (e.g. “set the table”)
  - To become firm, solid (chemical/physical: “cement sets”)
  - To fix a value or limit (“set a boundary”)

- **Noun**
  - Collection, suite (e.g. tea set, television set)
  - Mathematical set (formal definition: a collection of distinct objects)
  - Tennis/volleyball set (period of play)
  - Scenery/props arrangement in theatre/film (set design)
  - Arrangement of hair (cosmetic “hair set”)
  - Set in music/dance (sequence of tunes/moves)

#### **4. Regional and Domain-Specific Extensions**

- **Dialectal English**
  - To “set” in Yorkshire dialect means to plant (e.g. “set potatoes”)
  - In Scottish English: “set” = lease or agreement (land setting)

- **Professional Jargon**
  - Mining: “set” = frame supporting a tunnel
  - Printing: “set” = arrangement of type
  - Mathematics: "set" (noun) = fundamental concept in set theory (Cantor, late 19th c.)

- **Cultural and Technical Developments**
  - Technology: "set" as a device (e.g. "radio set")
  - Fashion: “set” (noun) for coordinated clothes
  - Biology: “set” as a group of eggs laid at one time
